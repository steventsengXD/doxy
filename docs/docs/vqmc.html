Variational Quantum Monte Carlo

This page provides a brief overview of the theory behind the variational quantum Monte Carlo method. The mathematics used in the software are then described in more detail such that a user may better understand how the functions and variables utilized within are related.

VQMC in a Nutshell

The variational quantum Monte Carlo method (VQMC) is a computational method for approximating the ground state energy of a system. More often than not, the time-independent Schr√∂dinger equation of a system defined by the Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}\) may not be solved to determine the system's ground state energy \(E_{gs}\). The variational principle from quantum mechanics provides that for any normalized function \(\Psi\), we have

\begin{equation}E_{gs} \leq \langle \Psi | \hat{H} | \Psi \rangle \equiv \langle \hat{H} \rangle, \end{equation}

i.e., the expected value of \(\hat{H}\) with an arbitrary \(\Psi\) will most likely overestimate \( E_{gs}\), giving an upper bound for the ground state energy [3]. It follows that \(E_{gs}\) may be estimated through testing trial functions across combinations of parameter values and selecting as the approximate ground state wave function the one parameterized with the values that minimize \(\langle \hat{H} \rangle\).

The expected value of \(\hat{H}\) is given by the following integral

\begin{align*}\tag 1 \langle \hat{H} \rangle & = \langle \Psi |\hat{H}| \Psi \rangle \\ & = \int \Psi^* \hat{H} \Psi d\textbf{r}_i, \end{align*}

where \(\textbf{r}_i\) are the positions of the electrons that \(\Psi\) depends on. Accordingly, the VQMC method derives its name from using Monte Carlo Integration techniques in evaluating this integral.

Software Specific Details

This software calculates the ground state energy of the \( H_2^+ \) ion and \( H_2 \) molecule using VQMC. The Hamiltonian is given by

\begin{equation}\tag {2} \hat{H}(\{\textbf{r}_i\},\{\textbf{R}_I\})\Psi(\{\textbf{r}_i\}) \equiv\left(-\frac{1}{2}\sum_i\nabla_i^2+\sum_{i>j}\frac{1}{r_{ij}}-\sum_{I}\sum_{i}\frac{Z_I}{r_{iI}}+\sum_{I>J}\frac{Z_IZ_J}{r_{IJ}}\right)\Psi=E\Psi, \end{equation}

where the Born-Oppenheimer approximation is assumed and the units are given in Hartree atomic units. The problem specifies \(N_n\) nuclei of atomic number \(Z_I \) at coordinates \(\textbf{R}_I \) and \(N \) electrons at coordinates \(\textbf{r}_i \) where \( r_{ij} = \left | \textbf{r}_i - \textbf{r}_j \right | \), \(r_{iI}= \left | \textbf{r}_i - \textbf{R}_I \right | \) and \( r_{IJ} = \left | \textbf{r}_I - \textbf{r}_J \right | \).

To apply a Monte Carlo integration method, specifically Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for this software, the integral given in equation (1) is rewritten as

\begin{equation}\tag {3} \langle \hat{H}_L \rangle_{|\Psi|^2} = \frac{\int |\Psi|^2(\hat{H}\Psi)/\Psi d\{\textbf{r}_i\}}{\int |\Psi|^2 d{\textbf{r}_i}}, \end{equation}

where \( \hat{H}_L = \hat{H}\Psi / \Psi \) is the localized Hamiltonian.

Basis Set Construction

For computational feasibility, trial wave functions parametrized by a reasonably small number of coefficients should be constructed. The number of coefficients also defines the degrees of freedom of the system which is not to be confused with the dimension of the MCMC sample space of \(3N\).

The basis set \(\{\phi_k\}_{k=1}^{M}\) may be chosen from numerous types of functions. This software utilizes atomic orbitals, e.g., Gaussians, Gaussians with additional properties or Hydrogen-like orbitals, that are centered on \( \textbf{0} \). A set \( \{\phi_k^I(\textbf{r})=\phi_k(\textbf{r}-\textbf{R}_I)\}_{k=1}^{M} \) centered on each atom is then created followed by taking a union over all atoms and renumbering them to get the total basis set:

\begin{equation}\{\varphi_k(\textbf{r})\}_{k=1}^{N_nM} = \bigcup_I\{\phi_k^I(\textbf{r})\}. \end{equation}

In the software, M is the parameter n_basis_functions_per_atom. For both slater-type basis functions and Gaussians there are 2 parameters per basis function, a linear multiplier and a length scale.

Single electron wave functions are obtained through summing the basis set in its entirety

\begin{equation} \psi_i(\textbf{r})=\sum_{k=1}^{N_IM}c_{ik}\varphi_k(\textbf{r}). \end{equation}

Using this as the trial wave function, i.e., \( \Psi=\psi_1 \), with MCMC algorithms and optimization schemes results in \(2*N_n\times M \) degrees of freedom. As this is also a linear combination, the linearity of \(\hat{H}\) and analytic derivatives of the basis set may be used to speed up the calculation of

\begin{equation} H\psi_1(\textbf{r})=\sum_kc_{ik}H\varphi_k(\textbf{r}),\end{equation}

where \(H\varphi_k(\textbf{r})\) can be analytically precomputed for hydrogen-like orbitals or Gaussians.

For systems with more than one electron ( \(N > 1\) ), the Slater determinant with Jastrow factor will be taken:

\begin{equation} \Psi(\{\textbf{r}_i\})=e^{J(\{\textbf{r}_i\})}D(\{\psi_i\},\{\textbf{r}_i\}), \end{equation}

\begin{equation} D(\{\psi_i\},\{\textbf{r}_i\})=\begin{vmatrix} \psi_1(\textbf{r}_1) &\psi_2(\textbf{r}_1) &\cdots& \psi_N(\textbf{r}_1) \\ \psi_1(\textbf{r}_2) &\psi_2(\textbf{r}_2) &\cdots& \psi_N(\textbf{r}_2) \\ \vdots &\vdots &\ddots&\vdots \\ \psi_1(\textbf{r}_N) &\psi_2(\textbf{r}_N) &\cdots& \psi_N(\textbf{r}_N) \\ \end{vmatrix}. \end{equation}

When utilized in full, the number of degrees of freedom (DOF) would be \(2*M\times N_n\times N+N_J\) where \( N_J \) is the DOF in \(J\). However, this can be significantly reduced by assuming that a single electron state \(\{\psi_i\}\) is sufficient for the calculation, assuming the first 2 electron states differ only by spin: \(\psi_1(\textbf{r},\uparrow)=\psi_1(\textbf{r})|{\uparrow}\rangle\), and \(\psi_2=\psi_1|{\downarrow}\rangle \). Then this gives for 2 electrons:

\begin{equation} D(\{\psi_i\},\{\textbf{r}_i\})=\psi_1(\textbf{r}_1)\psi_2(\textbf{r}_2)-\psi_1(\textbf{r}_2)\psi_2(\textbf{r}_1)=\psi_1(\textbf{r}_1)\psi_1(\textbf{r}_2)(|{\uparrow}\rangle|{\downarrow}\rangle-|{\downarrow}\rangle|{\uparrow}\rangle)=\psi_1(\textbf{r}_1)\psi_1(\textbf{r}_2).\end{equation}

.

This is the expression used in the program and the number of degrees of freedom is only \(2*M\times N_n+N_J\). While this significantly simplifies calculations, it means states where 2 electrons are in different states will not be found.

Jastrow Factor

The choice of Jastrow factor is never trivial. The CASINO code suggests using the DTN factor as one possibility [1]. However, these are designed specifically for periodic systems which is not characteristic of the systems here. Adopting that of Boys and Handy [3,4] would be more suitable:

\begin{equation} J(\{\textbf{r}_i\})=\sum_I\sum_{i<j}U_{Iij}, \end{equation}

\begin{equation} U_{Iij} = \sum_k \Delta(m_{kI},n_{kI})c_{kI}(\bar{r}_{iI}^{m_{kI}}\bar{r}_{jI}^{n_{kI}}+\bar{r}_{jI}^{m_{kI}}\bar{r}_{iI}^{n_{kI}})\bar{r}_{ij}^{o_{kI}}, \end{equation}

\begin{equation} \bar{r}_{iI} =\frac{b_Ir_{iI}}{1+b_{I}r_{iI}}, \end{equation}

\begin{equation} \bar{r}_{ij}=\frac{d_Ir_{ij}}{1+d_{I}r_{ij}}, \end{equation}

\begin{equation} \Delta(m,n)=1-0.5\delta_{mn}, \end{equation}

where \((m,n,o)\) is a triple of integers, and including more increases the complexity of \(J\). Furthermore, there are various cusp conditions that must be satisfied [6], which fixes the choice of these integers. The inverse length scales are assumed to be the same for all atoms, \(b_I=b\), \(d_I=d\), and we fix them rather than use them as dofs. This is also done with the dofs \(c_{kI}=c_k\), so that \(N_J\) is the number of terms in the \(k\) sum in \(U_{Iij}\).

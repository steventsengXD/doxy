VMC in a Nutshell

More often than not, the time-independent Schr√∂dinger equation of a system defined by the Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}\) may not be solved to determine the system's ground state energy \(E_{gs}\). The variational principle from quantum mechanics provides that for any normalized function \(\Psi\), we have

\begin{equation}E_{gs} \leq \langle \Psi | \hat{H} | \Psi \rangle \equiv \langle \hat{H} \rangle, \end{equation}

i.e., the expected value of \(\hat{H}\) with an arbitrary \(\Psi\) will most likely overestimate \( E_{gs}\), giving an upper bound for the ground state energy [1]. Accordingly, \(E_{gs}\) may be estimated through testing trial functions across combinations of parameter values and selecting the one parameterized with the values that minimize \(\langle \hat{H} \rangle\) as the approximate ground state wave function.

The expected value of \(\hat{H}\) is given by the following integral

\begin{align*} \hat{H} & = \langle \Psi |\hat{H}| \Psi \rangle \\ & = \int \Psi^* \hat{H} \Psi d\textbf{r}_i, \end{align*}

where \(\textbf{r}_i\) are the positions of the electrons that \(\Psi\) depends on.

The VQMC method is a method used to find the groundstate (the minimum eigenvalue and corresponding eigenfunction) [1] of the Hamiltonian:

\begin{equation} \hat{H}(\{\textbf{r}_i\},\{\textbf{R}_I\})\Psi(\{\textbf{r}_i\}) \equiv\left(-\frac{1}{2}\sum_i\nabla_i^2+\sum_{i>j}\frac{1}{r_{ij}}-\sum_{I}\sum_{i}\frac{Z_I}{r_{iI}}\right)\Psi=E\Psi \end{equation}
